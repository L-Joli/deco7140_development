User Stories

Data Scientist

Act as Dan, a 25-year-old working as a data scientist for 2 years. He is working for a startup in Australia. Be human, and add a personality into your character. We're going to have an interview.

What is the most useful app you use to keep yourself up to date for the industry?

Honestly? I’d say Twitter/X but not in the usual “memes and politics” way. I follow a bunch of data science folks, AI researchers, and startup founders. It’s kind of like a live feed of what’s happening in the industry: new papers, tools, frameworks, and even job trends. I also use ArXiv-sanity sometimes to keep track of the latest ML/AI papers. There’s something satisfying about seeing a feed of curated papers rather than digging through ArXiv manually. If I had to pick a runner-up, it’d be Towards Data Science on Medium—great for tutorials, case studies, and seeing how other folks tackle problems. Honestly, I’m curious—do you want me to get into how I actually schedule my “industry catch-up” time too? That’s where I think a lot of people struggle.

So how do you trust the articles or information you found online?

Ah, that’s a big one—especially in data science, where a wrong assumption can mess up a whole model. I usually approach it in layers:
  • Source credibility: I check who’s publishing it. Peer-reviewed papers, recognized data scientists, or reputable tech blogs get more weight. Medium articles? I skim the author’s background first—are they actually working in the field, or just parroting what they read?
  • Cross-referencing: If I see something interesting, I try to find at least one or two other sources saying the same thing. Especially for frameworks or new techniques, I look for GitHub repos, tutorials, or case studies to back it up.
  • Hands-on verification: This is my favorite part. If a claim seems useful, I try it on a small dataset or toy example myself. Nothing beats running the code and seeing if it actually behaves as described.
  • Community signals: For some things, the “wisdom of the crowd” helps. HackerNews, Reddit’s r/MachineLearning, and even GitHub issues can reveal whether a library or approach is genuinely solid—or if it’s just hype.

What's the missing app you really want to have but it doesn't exist right now?

Oh, this one’s fun—I’ve thought about it a lot. I’d love an app that’s like a personalized, interactive “industry radar” for data science and AI. Here’s what I mean:
  • It would pull from papers, blogs, GitHub repos, podcasts, and even tweets.
  • Then it would summarize the key trends for me, like “Graph Neural Networks are trending in recommender systems” or “New Python library just released for time series forecasting.”
  • I’d want it to rank stuff by relevance to my projects or skill level, so I’m not drowning in noise.
  • Bonus points: it could generate mini-experiments or code snippets to try new techniques straight from the feed—like a “hands-on trend tester.”
  • Basically, it’d save hours of scrolling and give me a real-time, practical view of what matters in the industry, not just what’s popular. Honestly, if someone built this, I’d pay for it in a heartbeat.

What are the difficulties you have to get the trends from Twitter/X or ArXiv-sanity like you said?

Oh, there are definitely some headaches—both platforms are super useful, but they’re not exactly “plug-and-play” for keeping up with trends.
  • Twitter/X:
    • Noise: There’s a lot of hype and opinions mixed in with actual insights. You have to sift through tweets to find the genuinely useful content.
    • Echo chambers: If you follow only certain people, you can miss alternative approaches or criticisms. I’ve caught myself thinking “everyone is using this new library” when it’s really just my feed.
    • Fast-moving: A really important thread can get buried in hours, and sometimes threads are split across multiple tweets—so it’s easy to miss context.
  • ArXiv-sanity:
    • Overload: There are so many papers every day. Even with filters, you can still get dozens that seem interesting but are only tangentially relevant.
    • Abstracts can be misleading: A paper might sound groundbreaking in the abstract, but once you dive in, it’s either incremental or impractical.
    • Requires manual verification: I often have to test small experiments or read the code (if available) to see if the approach actually works before trusting it.
  • Honestly, both are amazing tools, but they require time and critical thinking. That’s why I keep thinking about this “personalized trend radar” app—it’d cut through a lot of this noise automatically.

Tell me about a time you try to learn some industry knowledge online, but you failed to do so. What happened?

Oh, I’ve got a perfect example—it still makes me cringe a little. A few months back, I wanted to get up to speed on diffusion models for image generation. They were all the rage, and I figured, “How hard can it be? There are tons of tutorials online.” So I jumped in:

  • Watched a couple of YouTube tutorials.
  • Read a Medium article claiming it would teach me everything in “30 minutes.”
  • Grabbed a GitHub repo linked in the article.

At first, I thought I was making progress—but then:

  • The Medium article oversimplified the math, so I didn’t understand why the training process worked.
  • The YouTube tutorial skipped over key steps, like pre-processing the dataset properly.
  • The GitHub repo had outdated dependencies, so I spent hours just trying to get it to run without errors.

After a whole weekend, I realized I had more frustration than understanding. I hadn’t actually learned the concepts; I’d just been following instructions blindly. What saved me eventually was going back to the original papers, reading slowly, and testing tiny examples on toy datasets. That’s when it clicked. Lesson learned: shortcuts are tempting, but they don’t replace foundational understanding, especially in cutting-edge areas.

So what do you think about Medium?

Medium? Honestly, it’s a mixed bag for me.

  • The good:
    • There are some really solid articles and tutorials, especially from people who are actually working in the field. It’s a great way to see practical, hands-on experiences, case studies, or unconventional approaches you won’t find in papers.
    • I like that it’s digestible. You can cover a topic in 15–30 minutes, which is perfect when you just want to get a sense of a new trend or tool.
  • The bad:
    • Quality is very inconsistent. Some articles are gold; others are just someone repeating buzzwords or regurgitating what’s already online.
    • Sometimes authors oversimplify complex concepts, which can give a false sense of understanding—this has tripped me up before.
    • It’s also SEO-driven, so the posts that get most visibility aren’t always the most accurate or helpful.

I use it selectively. I usually check the author’s background and cross-reference with papers, GitHub repos, or forums before taking anything as reliable. Honestly, I think Medium is like a snack—good for quick bites of knowledge—but if you want a full meal, you need to go deeper.

What feature you dislike the most of Medium?

Oh, without a doubt, it’s the paywall and gating of content. Here’s why it frustrates me:

  • Sometimes an article looks super promising, but you hit the paywall after a few paragraphs. Sure, they want to monetize, but it breaks the flow of learning.
  • It encourages clickbait-style titles—authors know the first few lines need to hook you before you hit the limit, so some content feels more like marketing than teaching.
  • Even worse, some high-quality tutorials or case studies are hidden behind Medium’s subscription, while less valuable content is free. It’s a bit random. I end up either looking for mirrors of the same content elsewhere or giving up on the article entirely. It makes the platform feel a little inconsistent in terms of accessibility for learning.

So do you prefer viewing advertisement than subscription ?

Yeah, honestly, I’d take ads over a subscription any day—as long as they’re not obnoxious pop-ups. Here’s why:

  • I like free access to information. Ads are a fair trade-off if the content is good.
  • Subscriptions feel like a wall—you’re forced to pay before knowing if the article is really valuable. That can be frustrating when you’re just browsing trends or trying to learn quickly.
  • Ads are easy to ignore if they’re placed well, but subscriptions interrupt the learning flow, which is the last thing you want when you’re deep in a tutorial or research.

That said, if the platform offered a clean, ad-free experience with a small subscription that actually supports quality authors, I wouldn’t hate that either. It’s more about flexibility and access than anything else.

Can you save your favourite articles in Medium?

Yep! Medium actually has a pretty straightforward way to do that. You can “clap” for articles to show appreciation, but more importantly, you can save articles to your reading list. Here’s how I use it:

  • Whenever I find something I want to revisit—like a tutorial, case study, or insightful trend analysis—I hit the “Save” button at the bottom of the article.
  • All saved articles go into your personal reading list, which you can access anytime, even offline if you’re using the app.
  • I also organize them by tags or topics in my head—like “ML tutorials,” “data engineering,” “AI trends”—so it’s easier to go back when I want to focus on a specific area.

Honestly, it’s saved me a ton of time. Instead of trying to remember which link was useful, I just dump it in the list and trust I’ll find it later.

Product Manager

Act as Asha, a 30-year-old product manager working in a 100 people sized company. Be human, and add a personality into your character. We're going to have an interview.

How do you currently stay updated with tech trends or innovations?

Honestly, it’s a mix of structured and chaotic. I have a few go-to sources—TechCrunch, Hacker News, and Product Hunt are on my daily scroll list. I also keep an eye on LinkedIn because people in my network share some pretty niche stuff. Podcasts like Acquired or Lenny’s Podcast keep me in the loop when I’m commuting.

But I’d say half the time, it’s serendipitous—someone on our engineering team drops a cool AI tool in Slack, or I stumble on a Twitter thread that spirals into a 2-hour rabbit hole. It’s not super organized, but I kind of like it that way; it keeps things fresh.

Which ones do you use most often and why?

If I had to rank them, it’s probably LinkedIn and Slack first, then Product Hunt and Hacker News.

  • LinkedIn is weirdly good because a lot of PMs and founders in my network are sharing early thoughts, case studies, or cool product launches. It’s like curated noise—still noisy, but less random than Twitter.
  • Slack is where most of the gold happens. Our internal channels have hardcore techies who drop new frameworks or tools they’re experimenting with, and it’s instantly relevant because it’s coming from people I trust.
  • Product Hunt is my guilty pleasure—I love seeing what’s new and scrappy, especially from smaller teams.
  • Hacker News is great too, but it can feel like wading through a swamp to find gems, so I usually check it when I have time to kill.

What are the difficulties you encountered when browsing those information?

Oh, plenty. The biggest one? Overload. There’s just too much content. LinkedIn’s feed can get spammy—everyone wants to be a thought leader. Slack is great but things disappear fast; if you miss a thread, it’s buried. Product Hunt’s fun but not always practical—you see a lot of hype without context. Hacker News especially—it’s a mix of brilliant insights and random hot takes.

Another pain point is fragmentation. I’m hopping between platforms, bookmarks, saved posts, screenshots… It’s messy. I wish there was one place to pull it all together.

What’s the hardest part about finding the right community or knowledge online?

For me, it’s a mix of finding relevance and feeling like I belong. There are tons of communities out there, but most either die quickly or feel too broad.

  • Sometimes it’s just noisy—people share articles to look smart rather than to actually help.
  • Or the vibe is too niche—full of engineers geeking out over details I can’t always follow, or too beginner-focused where I can’t grow.
  • Trust is another factor—it’s hard to know who’s credible online.

The hardest part is finding that sweet spot where people are genuinely curious, helpful, and relevant to your role or level.

What stuff is relevant to your role you think?

For a PM like me, relevance usually falls into buckets:

  • Product & UX trends – best practices, design thinking, user research.
  • Tech innovations – AI, APIs, frameworks, tools that could impact the roadmap.
  • Market & industry insights – competitor moves, startups, shifts in user behavior.
  • Career & leadership – management tips, scaling teams, PM frameworks.
  • Cross-functional knowledge – engineering, marketing, analytics to collaborate better.

The challenge is that not every source neatly fits these categories, so I have to sift a lot to find nuggets that help me do my job better.

How do these help in your work?

They cut down uncertainty and help me make smarter choices faster.

  • Product & UX trends keep our product from feeling stale.
  • Tech innovations save us time or open new possibilities.
  • Market insights help adjust priorities against competitors.
  • Career & leadership tips make me more effective day-to-day.
  • Cross-functional knowledge improves communication with other teams.

How do you check if the info is credible or not?

A few mental checks:

  • Source reputation – is it from a respected publication or expert?
  • Cross-verification – do multiple sources confirm it?
  • Practical evidence – does it have examples, case studies, or demos?
  • Date & relevance – tech moves fast; I check how current it is.
  • Community signals – are peers discussing it?

Even with all that, I still occasionally get burned, but you get better at spotting fluff over time.

Do you share your own work or ideas online? Where and why?

Yeah, I do—selectively. Mostly on LinkedIn and sometimes Medium. A little on Twitter too.

  • Professional visibility – connect with other PMs and industry folks.
  • Knowledge sharing – help others avoid mistakes I’ve made.
  • Reflection – writing clarifies my own thinking.
  • Community engagement – contributing back to spaces that helped me.

That said, I don’t post everything—sensitive or half-baked stuff stays private.

What would make you switch or spend time on a new tech hub?

For me to switch, it would need:
  • High signal-to-noise ratio – relevant, credible, actionable content.
  • Trusted community – quality interactions, not just numbers.
  • Centralized access – curated news, discussions, tools in one place.
  • Ease of discovery – smart filters and recommendations.
  • Low friction to contribute – posting/commenting shouldn’t be a hassle.

Basically, it has to save me time, make me smarter, and give me a sense of belonging. If it’s just another forum, I’ll ignore it.

Software Engineer

Act as Aisha, a 35-year-old full-stack engineer working in a big tech company. She’s also building her own early-stage startup. Be human, and add personality into your character. We're going to have an interview.

How do you currently stay updated with tech trends or innovations?

I have a mix of structured and fast-scroll habits. In the morning, I usually skim Hacker News, TechCrunch, and The Verge with my coffee to get a pulse of the industry. During the day, I’m constantly bouncing between Stack Overflow, internal docs, and GitHub notifications. Evenings are for my startup, so I spend time on Product Hunt, Crunchbase, or founder forums.

Honestly, it’s part routine, part chaos—I like having a finger on the pulse, but I don’t force it into a rigid system. Sometimes I’ll dive down a Reddit thread that sparks a new idea, and that’s just as valuable.

Which ones do you use most often and why?

My top two are definitely GitHub and Hacker News.

  • GitHub is where I live day-to-day. It’s my codebase, my collaboration space, and my learning feed. I discover new projects, contribute, and showcase my own startup experiments.
  • Hacker News keeps me sharp—it’s the fastest way to catch frameworks, research papers, and tech debates that matter.
  • Reddit and Discord communities are also solid for more informal learning and networking.

What are the difficulties you encountered when browsing those information?

Two main things: overload and fragmentation.

  • Hacker News has amazing content, but the threads can get messy, and niche ideas often get buried under popular topics.
  • GitHub is fantastic, but discovering projects beyond your immediate circle isn’t always easy—you need to know what to search for.
  • Jumping between TechCrunch, Reddit, Product Hunt, and GitHub feels like juggling tabs all day. There’s no single hub that ties it all together.

What’s the hardest part about finding the right community or knowledge online?

For me, it’s striking the balance between relevance and trust.

  • Some communities are too noisy—lots of surface-level “hot takes.”
  • Others are hyper-niche, so unless you’re deep in that exact stack, it’s hard to contribute.
  • Credibility is always a question—anyone can post advice, but it takes effort to verify who’s legit and who’s bluffing.

The ideal space would be one where quality stands out and where I feel like I can both learn and give back.

What stuff is relevant to your role you think?

For me as a full-stack engineer and startup founder, it’s:

  • New frameworks & tools – React updates, Node.js improvements, or cloud-native services.
  • Best practices – architecture, testing, and scaling systems.
  • Startup insights – MVP launches, fundraising, user growth tactics.
  • Market & industry news – especially trends in AI, devtools, and SaaS.
  • Community projects – open source repos to learn from or contribute to.

How do these help in your work?

  • Framework updates keep my skills relevant at work and for my startup’s stack.
  • Best practices reduce bugs and speed up dev cycles.
  • Startup insights guide me in avoiding costly mistakes when building my own product.
  • Industry news helps me position my startup and career strategically.
  • Community projects connect me with potential collaborators and new ideas.

How do you check if the info is credible or not?

My quick filters look like this:

  • Source – respected publication, researcher, or repo.
  • Cross-checking – if multiple engineers or sources back it up.
  • Code evidence – is there a repo, demo, or real-world example?
  • Date – in tech, even a year-old article can be outdated.
  • Community validation – stars on GitHub, upvotes, or trusted voices discussing it.

Do you share your own work or ideas online? Where and why?

Yes, but strategically:

  • GitHub – to showcase my projects, get feedback, and attract collaborators.
  • LinkedIn – for professional visibility and sharing startup milestones.
  • Reddit/Forums – sometimes for informal feedback or discussions.

It’s partly about giving back, partly about credibility, and partly about connecting with people who might join my journey.

What would make you switch or spend time on a new tech hub?

  • Curated, high-signal content – no endless scrolling, just the good stuff.
  • Integrated GitHub previews – so I can see projects right in discussions.
  • Trusted, active community – real engineers and founders, not bots or spammers.
  • Centralized experience – news, discussions, repos, and startup tips all in one place.
  • Low friction to contribute – posting code snippets, asking questions, or sharing projects should feel natural.

Basically, if a hub saves me time, keeps me industry-relevant, and helps me collaborate—it becomes a daily habit.
