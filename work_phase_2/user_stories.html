So how do you trust the articles or information you found online?

Ah, that’s a big one—especially in data science, where a wrong assumption can mess up a whole model. I usually approach it in layers: 1. **Source credibility:** I check who’s publishing it. Peer-reviewed papers, recognized data scientists, or reputable tech blogs get more weight. Medium articles? I skim the author’s background first—are they actually working in the field, or just parroting what they read? 2. **Cross-referencing:** If I see something interesting, I try to find at least one or two other sources saying the same thing. Especially for frameworks or new techniques, I look for GitHub repos, tutorials, or case studies to back it up. 3. **Hands-on verification:** This is my favorite part. If a claim seems useful, I try it on a small dataset or toy example myself. Nothing beats running the code and seeing if it actually behaves as described. 4. **Community signals:** For some things, the “wisdom of the crowd” helps. HackerNews, Reddit’s r/MachineLearning, and even GitHub issues can reveal whether a library or approach is genuinely solid—or if it’s just hype. Honestly, I think being skeptical is half the job. If something sounds too good to be true, I treat it as experimental until proven otherwise. what are the difficulties you have to get the trends from Twitter/X or ArXiv-sanity like you said?

Oh, there are definitely some headaches—both platforms are super useful, but they’re not exactly “plug-and-play” for keeping up with trends. **Twitter/X:** * **Noise:** There’s a lot of hype and opinions mixed in with actual insights. You have to sift through tweets to find the genuinely useful content. * **Echo chambers:** If you follow only certain people, you can miss alternative approaches or criticisms. I’ve caught myself thinking “everyone is using this new library” when it’s really just my feed. * **Fast-moving:** A really important thread can get buried in hours, and sometimes threads are split across multiple tweets—so it’s easy to miss context. **ArXiv-sanity:** * **Overload:** There are *so many papers* every day. Even with filters, you can still get dozens that seem interesting but are only tangentially relevant. * **Abstracts can be misleading:** A paper might sound groundbreaking in the abstract, but once you dive in, it’s either incremental or impractical. * **Requires manual verification:** I often have to test small experiments or read the code (if available) to see if the approach actually works before trusting it. Honestly, both are amazing tools, but they **require time and critical thinking**. That’s why I keep thinking about this “personalized trend radar” app—it’d cut through a lot of this noise automatically. At first, I thought I was making progress—but then: * The Medium article **oversimplified the math**, so I didn’t understand why the training process worked. * The YouTube tutorial **skipped over key steps**, like pre-processing the dataset properly. * The GitHub repo **had outdated dependencies**, so I spent hours just trying to get it to run without errors. After a whole weekend, I realized I had **more frustration than understanding**. I hadn’t actually learned the concepts; I’d just been following instructions blindly. What saved me eventually was going back to the **original papers**, reading slowly, and testing tiny examples on toy datasets. That’s when it clicked. Lesson learned: shortcuts are tempting, but they don’t replace **foundational understanding**, especially in cutting-edge areas. So what do you think about Medium?

Medium? Honestly, it’s a mixed bag for me. **The good:** * There are some really solid articles and tutorials, especially from people who are actually working in the field. It’s a great way to see **practical, hands-on experiences**, case studies, or unconventional approaches you won’t find in papers. * I like that it’s **digestible**. You can cover a topic in 15–30 minutes, which is perfect when you just want to get a sense of a new trend or tool. **The bad:** * Quality is **very inconsistent**. Some articles are gold; others are just someone repeating buzzwords or regurgitating what’s already online. * Sometimes authors **oversimplify complex concepts**, which can give a false sense of understanding—this has tripped me up before. * It’s also **SEO-driven**, so the posts that get most visibility aren’t always the most accurate or helpful. I use it selectively. I usually check the author’s background and cross-reference with papers, GitHub repos, or forums before taking anything as reliable. Honestly, I think Medium is like a snack—good for quick bites of knowledge—but if you want a full meal, you need to go deeper. If you want, I can tell you **how I personally filter Medium articles so I don’t waste time on the fluff**. That method actually saves a lot of frustration.

what feature you dislike the most of Medium?

Oh, without a doubt, it’s the **paywall and gating of content**. Here’s why it frustrates me: * Sometimes an article looks super promising, but you hit the paywall after a few paragraphs. Sure, they want to monetize, but it **breaks the flow of learning**. * It encourages **clickbait-style titles**—authors know the first few lines need to hook you before you hit the limit, so some content feels more like marketing than teaching. * Even worse, some high-quality tutorials or case studies are **hidden behind Medium’s subscription**, while less valuable content is free. It’s a bit random. I end up either looking for mirrors of the same content elsewhere or giving up on the article entirely. It makes the platform feel a little inconsistent in terms of accessibility for learning. If you want, I can share the **one workaround I use to still get value from Medium without paying for everything**—it’s kind of a life hack for data science reading.

so do you prefer viewing advertisement than subscription ?

Yeah, honestly, I’d take ads over a subscription any day—**as long as they’re not obnoxious pop-ups**. Here’s why: * I like **free access** to information. That can be frustrating when you’re just browsing trends or trying to learn quickly. * Ads are easy to ignore if they’re placed well, but subscriptions **interrupt the learning flow**, which is the last thing you want when you’re deep in a tutorial or research. That said, if the platform offered **a clean, ad-free experience with a small subscription** that actually supports quality authors, I wouldn’t hate that either. It’s more about **flexibility and access** than anything else.
